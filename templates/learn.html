What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate trusted organizations (banks, tech companies, government agencies) to trick victims into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or Social Security numbers.

The term comes from "fishing" — attackers cast a wide net hoping someone "bites."

📊

Types of Phishing Attacks

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating brands. Most common type.

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks using personal info about the victim.

Smishing

Phishing via SMS text messages.

Vishing

Voice/phone call phishing attacks.

Clone Phishing

Duplicating a real email with malicious links.

Whaling

Targeting high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs).

10 Red Flags to Spot Phishing

  1. Urgency & Threats — "Act NOW or your account will be deleted!"
  2. Misspelled domains — paypa1.com instead of paypal.com
  3. Generic greetings — "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  4. Suspicious links — Hover before you click. Does the URL match?
  5. Unexpected attachments — Never open unknown .exe, .zip, .docm files
  6. Requests for sensitive info — Legit orgs never ask for passwords by email
  7. Poor grammar/spelling — Professional orgs proofread their emails
  8. Too-good-to-be-true offers — "You've won $1,000,000!"
  9. Mismatched email headers — "From" address doesn't match the company
  10. Pressure to bypass security — "Don't tell IT, this is confidential"

How ML Detects Phishing

Machine learning models extract features from emails and messages, then classify them as phishing or legitimate. Common features include:

Urgency keyword frequency URL structure analysis Domain reputation Text entropy Capitalization ratio Sensitive info requests Sender domain mismatch Punctuation patterns

Models like Random Forest, Naive Bayes, and BERT are commonly used in production phishing detectors.

✅

Best Practices to Stay Safe

  • Never click links in unsolicited emails — type URLs directly
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) everywhere
  • Use a password manager — unique passwords for every site
  • Keep software & antivirus updated
  • Report phishing emails to your IT team or email provider
  • When in doubt, call the organization directly using a number from their official website
